Notes & Learnings

Things I
Write Down

Data Science · Personal Learning

What I learned while designing a decision intelligence system

When I started HDIS, I thought the hardest part would be building the AI. It wasn't. The hardest part was defining what the data should look like.

I had to think carefully about every column: what is a "decision"? How do you encode "confidence"? This forced me to practice feature engineering before writing a single model.

The second big lesson: EDA before modeling, always. When I ran exploratory analysis on my decision records, patterns appeared I didn't expect — certain categories had far more failures. That's data science doing its real job.

Building HDIS taught me: the data pipeline is where intelligence actually lives — not in the algorithm.

AI / ML · Opinion

Why prediction alone is not enough in AI

Most ML courses teach you to maximize accuracy. But after studying decision systems and completing the Tata GenAI simulation, I started asking: accurate for whom, and then what?

A model can be 92% accurate and completely useless in practice — because it doesn't tell the user why it predicted something, or what to do with that prediction.

This is why Explainable AI (XAI) matters to me. A data scientist's job isn't done when the model is trained. It's done when a real person can make a better decision because of it.

Prediction is the beginning. Decision support is the goal.

Learning · Honest Reflection

Mistakes I made while learning data science

Mistake 1: Jumping to models before understanding the data. I used to run classifiers on raw datasets before any EDA. The models always gave terrible results — and I blamed the algorithm, not my process.

Mistake 2: Treating accuracy as the only metric. A model predicting the majority class can hit 95% accuracy on imbalanced data. I learned about precision, recall, and F1 the hard way.

Mistake 3: Not documenting. I solved problems, moved on, forgot how. Now I document everything — failed experiments included.

The biggest shift: confusion is not failure. It is the actual process of learning data science.
